Brain Tumors

Despite popular belief, there is no one form of brain tumor, and not all brain tumors are cancerous. Cancerous tumors are malignant in nature. They grow rapidly and aggressively, and can also spread to other parts of the body.

Tumors that do not invade other healthy tissues of the body are called benign tumors. Benign tumors do not present the same risks as malignant tumors, but they can still impair the brain’s normal functioning.

Primary brain tumors originate in the brain itself, whereas metastatic brain tumors are caused due to cancer cells spreading to the brain through blood or lymph vessels from other parts of the body.

Physical, Emotional, and Social Effects of a Brain Tumor:

Being diagnosed with a brain tumor is stressful to say the least. It leads to emotional and physical implications that can affect a person’s personal life.

What are some of the possible risks or complications that can lead to Brain tumor?

Brain tumors can potentially lead to a number of medical complications. These complications can be mild, or in some instances, life-altering.

Almost 50% of people diagnosed with a brain tumor complain about chronic headaches due to pressure on nearby nerves and blood vessels.

  • Age: Brain tumors can occur at any age, though risk increases with age.
  • Exposure to radiation: Radiotherapy, CT scans, and X-rays increase risk.
  • Obesity: Higher BMI may increase susceptibility to meningioma.
  • Other risk factors: Mobile phones, alcohol, power lines, and hair dyes.
